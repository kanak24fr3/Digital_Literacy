Social Media Do's and Don'ts Checklist


Do's
	Don'ts
	Do keep your privacy settings updated to control who sees your personal information and posts.
	Don't post sensitive information like your home address, phone number, or current live location.
	Do think before you post; consider how a future employer or professor might view your content.
	Don't engage in "cyber-arguments" or use offensive language, even if you are provoked.
	Do verify information from multiple credible sources before sharing news or "viral" stories.
	Don't post photos or videos of others without their explicit consent, especially in private settings.
	Do use your platform to showcase your skills, projects (like your B.Tech work), and achievements.
	Don't complain about your college, professors, or internship supervisors on public forums.
	Do practice "digital empathy" by treating others with the same respect you would in person.
	Don't plagiarize content or claim someone else's digital work/code as your own.
	



The Impact of Poor Digital Communication
A notable real-world problem occurs when students use informal "text-speak" or an aggressive tone in professional settings, such as a hypothetical scenario where a student sends a demanding, one-line email to a professor regarding a low grade. In this case, the student’s lack of a professional greeting and clear context led the professor to perceive them as disrespectful, resulting in a strained relationship and a refusal to discuss the grade further.
This situation could have been handled differently by applying basic email etiquette. The student should have used a clear subject line, a professional salutation, and a polite request for a meeting to understand their mistakes. By focusing on "why" they lost marks rather than "demanding" a change, the communication would have remained constructive. This highlights that in the digital world, your tone and structure are just as important as your message in maintaining professional credibility.
